Multi-Factor Security

November 29, 2024

A security system that requires more than one form of verification to authenticate a user.

Description

Multi-Factor Security (MFS) is an authentication mechanism that enhances the security of identity and access management systems by requiring users to present multiple pieces of evidence when logging in. Typically, this involves a combination of at least two of the following factors: something you know (a password or PIN), something you have (a security token or smartphone), and something you are (biometric verification such as fingerprints or facial recognition). The goal of MFS is to provide a layered defense, making it more difficult for unauthorized users to gain access to a target. By requiring multiple forms of identification, MFS significantly reduces the risk of identity theft, phishing attacks, and unauthorized access, even if one factor is compromised. This additional verification step ensures that the person trying to gain access is genuinely who they claim to be, thus enhancing overall security. MFS is widely adopted in both consumer and enterprise environments, especially for protecting sensitive data and systems.

Examples

  • Using a password and a fingerprint scan to access a mobile banking app.
  • Entering a PIN sent to your phone after logging in with your username and password on an online service.

Additional Information

  • MFS can be implemented with various combinations of authentication factors depending on the security needs.
  • Organizations often use Multi-Factor Security to comply with regulatory requirements for data protection.
