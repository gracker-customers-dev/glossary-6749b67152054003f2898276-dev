Multi-Factor Authentication

November 29, 2024

A security process that requires users to provide two or more verification factors to gain access to a resource.

Description

Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA) is a security mechanism that enhances protection by requiring users to present two or more separate forms of verification before gaining access to a system, application, or resource. Typically, these factors include something the user knows (like a password), something the user has (such as a smartphone or hardware token), and something the user is (biometrics like a fingerprint). The primary goal of MFA is to create a layered defense and make it more difficult for unauthorized access to occur. If one factor is compromised, the attacker would still have at least one more barrier to breach before successfully gaining access. MFA is widely used in both consumer and enterprise environments to protect sensitive data and systems, reduce the risk of fraud, and comply with regulatory requirements. Its adoption is a critical component of modern identity and access management strategies, providing an effective means to safeguard against identity theft and unauthorized access.

Examples

  • Example 1: A user logging into a bank account must enter a password and then confirm a code sent to their smartphone.
  • Example 2: Accessing a corporate VPN requires both a password and a fingerprint scan.

Additional Information

  • Additional info 1: MFA can significantly reduce the risk of account breaches and is recommended for securing sensitive information.
  • Additional info 2: Many services now offer app-based MFA options, which can be more convenient and secure than SMS-based codes.
